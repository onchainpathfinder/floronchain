MiCA Explained: What Every Crypto User in Europe Needs to Know Right Now
10–12 minAll levelsCoin Bureau style
Primary keyword: MiCA regulation explained  ·  Secondary: Markets in Crypto Assets regulation, MiCA stablecoin rules, MiCA crypto exchange requirements, EU crypto regulation 2025  ·  Intent: Informational
HOOK — 0:00–0:12 · Camera "The European Union just created the most comprehensive crypto regulation framework in the world. It's called MiCA. Here's what it actually says, and what it actually means." 📱 TEXT: MiCA — the biggest crypto regulation in history WHAT IS MiCA — 0:12–2:00 · Camera "MiCA stands for Markets in Crypto Assets Regulation. It's a European Union law that creates a single regulatory framework for crypto across all 27 EU member states. Before MiCA, every country in Europe had its own rules — or no rules at all. MiCA harmonises everything." "It came into full force at the end of 2024. If you're operating a crypto business anywhere in the EU — or you're an EU citizen using crypto — you're operating under MiCA right now." 📱 TEXT: MiCA — fully in force since December 2024 "And here's what makes MiCA significant globally: it's the first major jurisdiction to pass comprehensive crypto regulation. The US still hasn't. The UK is working on it. The EU got there first, and everyone else is watching." WHO DOES IT AFFECT — 2:00–4:00 · Camera · Screen graphics help here "MiCA covers three main groups. First — crypto asset issuers. If you're launching a token in the EU, you now need to publish a white paper that meets specific legal requirements. No more anonymous launches with no accountability." "Second — Crypto Asset Service Providers, or CASPs. This is exchanges, custodians, brokers, portfolio managers — anyone providing crypto services in the EU. They need a MiCA licence. Without it, they can't operate legally in the EU." "Third — stablecoin issuers. This is the strictest part. If you issue a stablecoin in the EU, you need to meet reserve requirements, redemption rights, and transaction limits. Tether was specifically in the crosshairs here — and several exchanges delisted USDT for EU customers as a direct result." 📱 TEXT: USDT delisted by some EU exchanges because of MiCA stablecoin rules KEY REQUIREMENTS — 4:00–6:30 · Camera + screen if useful "Let me give you the key requirements that matter in practice." "For exchanges and service providers: you need a CASP licence from your national regulator. Once you have it, you get a EU passport — you can operate across all 27 member states. This is actually good news for legitimate businesses. One licence, one market." "For stablecoin issuers: if your stablecoin transacts more than one million times a day or reaches one billion euros in daily volume — it gets flagged as a significant stablecoin and faces tighter supervision directly from the European Banking Authority." 📱 TEXT: Significant stablecoins → supervised directly by the EBA "For token issuers: you need a white paper with specific disclosures — what the token is, what it's for, what the risks are. You're liable for misleading information. The anonymous no-accountability token launch is over in Europe." "And for consumers: you now have more protection. Exchanges must segregate your funds from their own. They must have clear complaints procedures. They can't use misleading marketing. And if they lose your assets, there are liability rules." WHAT'S NOT COVERED — 6:30–7:30 · Camera "Now — what MiCA doesn't cover, because this confuses a lot of people." "MiCA does not cover NFTs — most of them. It doesn't cover truly decentralised DeFi protocols where there's no identifiable issuer. It doesn't cover central bank digital currencies. And it doesn't fully address DAOs." "The EU knows these are gaps. There's already discussion of a MiCA 2 that would address DeFi and NFTs. But for now — those spaces remain largely unregulated under MiCA." 📱 TEXT: DeFi + NFTs = mostly outside MiCA. For now. FLOR'S TAKE — 7:30–9:00 · Camera · Honest practitioner view "Here's my honest take after working in regulated crypto markets in the EU." "MiCA is a genuine step forward. It creates legal certainty that didn't exist before. For legitimate crypto businesses, that's valuable — you know what you need to do, and if you do it, you have a clear path to market. The EU passport is a real commercial advantage." "The challenges are implementation speed and regulatory capacity. National regulators are still building the teams and systems to process CASP licence applications properly. In some member states, the practical capacity to deal with MiCA is still catching up with the legal requirements." "And the stablecoin situation is genuinely disruptive. Losing access to USDT — which is the most widely used stablecoin in the world — created real friction for EU traders and businesses. The long-term intention is to push the market toward EU-compliant stablecoins, but in the short term it's been painful." 📱 TEXT: "MiCA is a step forward. But the transition has been painful."
