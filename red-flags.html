🚩 Crypto AML Red Flags Library

The typologies your transaction monitoring should catch — grouped by where they show up. Not exhaustive, not legal advice. The honest list of what actually matters when you're triaging an alert at 2pm on a Friday.

Last updated: April 2026 · Maintained by Flor
01

Customer Behaviour

▶New account, high-value activity
Account opened recently and immediately moves significant value — bypasses the customer risk baseline that builds over time.
ActionTrigger EDD. Re-verify ID, request source of funds and source of wealth documentation.
▶Evasive on KYC
Customer becomes aggressive, reluctant or refuses to provide standard documentation when asked. Inconsistent with a legitimate profile.
ActionNote the interaction in the case file. Treat as an EDD trigger; consider account restriction pending review.
▶Geolocation inconsistent with KYC
IP location does not match the stated country of residence — possible identity misuse, account takeover or use of a money mule.
ActionTrigger step-up authentication. Review prior login history and document the mismatch.
▶Profile mismatch
Stated occupation, age or income bracket cannot plausibly support the volume or frequency of transactions observed.
ActionRequest updated source-of-funds evidence and recalibrate the customer risk score.
02

Transaction Patterns

▶Structuring (smurfing)
Transactions broken into multiple sub-threshold amounts to stay below reporting limits — classic smurfing.
ActionAggregate the transaction chain in monitoring rules. Escalate to the MLRO and document the pattern for STR.
▶Rapid layering
Short dwell time between deposit and withdrawal. Funds move in and out within minutes or hours — consistent with placement-layering.
ActionActivate transaction-velocity alerts. Apply a 72-hour hold for MLRO review on similar patterns.
▶Round-dollar deposits
Patterns of suspiciously round amounts (e.g. exactly $9,500 or $4,999) — typical of someone calibrating to thresholds.
ActionFlag for manual review. Cross-reference with the customer's stated business model.
▶Sudden volume spike on a dormant account
An account that has been quiet for months suddenly handles significant value. Often the layering phase of a larger scheme.
ActionRe-verify the customer, document the change, and check for compromised credentials.
03

Counterparty Risk

▶Mixer / privacy-tool exposure
Funds routed through Tornado Cash, Wasabi, Samurai or any coinjoin service — deliberate obfuscation of transaction history.
ActionFile an STR with the FIU. Freeze the transaction pending MLRO review. Consider account closure for repeat exposure.
▶Flagged or sanctioned address
Interaction with a wallet on a watchlist (Chainalysis, Elliptic, OFAC SDN, EU consolidated sanctions list).
ActionBlock the counterparty, screen the customer, file an STR within statutory deadlines.
▶PEP or sanctions exposure
Politically Exposed Person or sanctioned individual involved as customer or counterparty — inherent higher-risk profile.
ActionObtain senior-management approval for the relationship. Apply ongoing enhanced monitoring.
▶High-risk jurisdiction
Activity linked to a FATF grey or black-listed country or an EU AML-listed third country (e.g. Iran, DPRK, Myanmar).
ActionApply EDD under FATF Recommendation 19 / AMLD6 Article 18. Document rationale in the customer file.
▶No-KYC / pseudonymous counterparty
Transacting with an unhosted wallet or unregulated P2P platform — Travel Rule data unavailable for the leg.
ActionApply EDD on the customer. Block if Travel Rule data is unobtainable and required by your jurisdiction.
▶Third-party payment
Funds moving through wallets unrelated to the named customer — possible straw buyer or layering tier.
ActionVerify beneficial ownership and the documented relationship between parties before release.
04

Crypto-Specific

▶Cross-chain hopping
Bridging between blockchains within a short window — obscures the audit trail and is a known layering technique.
ActionTrace the full chain using onchain analytics (Chainalysis, Elliptic, Arkham). Document the bridge path.
▶NFT wash trade pattern
Buyer and seller wallets share funding source, trade NFTs at inflated prices, no genuine secondary market activity.
ActionDo not rely on stated value. Refer to the NFT laundering playbook and document the cluster in the STR.
▶Flash-loan self-trade
Same actor on both sides of a transaction made possible by a flash loan — price reflects manipulation, not market.
ActionReject any value-based filing limit. Refer to market-abuse team and capture in STR narrative.
▶Freshly funded wallet, immediate large activity
Wallet created and funded in the same block as a high-value transaction — no transaction history, no reputation, no use case.
ActionTreat as untrusted counterparty. Hold for review and onchain trace.
▶Privacy-coin conversion
Conversion to or from Monero, Zcash or other privacy coins — frequently a layering step.
ActionApply EDD. Many regulated CASPs are obligated to refuse privacy-coin counterparties under MiCA.
